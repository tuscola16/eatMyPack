# Ultra Running Nutrition Strategy: Research & Data

## Calories Per Hour: Target Ranges

### The Core Challenge
Caloric expenditure during ultra running always exceeds what can be absorbed. The goal is to minimize the deficit — not eliminate it. The digestive system can only process a fraction of what muscles burn.

### Calorie Expenditure vs. Intake

| Effort Level | Cal Burned/Hour | Cal Target/Hour | Net Deficit/Hour |
|---|---|---|---|
| Easy ultra pace (12–15 min/mi) | 400–600 | 200–300 | 200–300 |
| Moderate pace (10–12 min/mi) | 500–700 | 250–350 | 250–400 |
| Hard effort (8–10 min/mi) | 600–900 | 150–250 | 400–650 |
| Mountain ultra (heavy climbing) | 500–800 | 200–350 | 250–500 |

> Note: Intensity directly limits what the gut can absorb. At hard efforts, less blood flows to the digestive system. Back off pace when GI distress starts.

### Evidence-Based Intake Targets

Research from 100-mile ultramarathon studies (including a 2024 *Scientific Reports* study) shows:

| Group | Avg Cal/Hour | Avg Carb/Hour | Completion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Elite finishers | 333 | ~83g | Yes |
| General finishers | 250–300 | ~70g | Yes |
| Non-finishers | <200 | <45g | No |

**Key stat**: Finishers consumed approximately 4.6 kcal/kg/h; non-finishers averaged 2.5 kcal/kg/h.

**Carbohydrate intake is the single strongest dietary predictor of race completion.**

### Recommended Ranges by Race Distance

| Distance | Duration | Cal/Hour Target | Carb/Hour Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50K | 4–8 hours | 150–250 | 40–60g |
| 50 Mile | 7–14 hours | 200–300 | 60–80g |
| 100K | 10–20 hours | 200–300 | 60–80g |
| 100 Mile | 15–36 hours | 250–350 | 70–90g |
| 200 Mile / Multi-day | 60–120+ hours | 200–300 | 60–90g |

**ISSN Position Stand** (International Society of Sports Nutrition): Recommends 60–90g carbohydrate/hour for ultra events, consistent with standard endurance guidance. Some trained athletes can absorb up to 120g/hour using 2:1 maltodextrin:fructose ratios.

## Nutrition Strategy by Race Phase

### Phase 1: First 1–3 Hours (Early Race / Fresh Legs)
- **Priority**: Carbohydrates
- **Format**: Gels, chews, drink mix
- **Target**: 60–80g carbs/hour
- **Gut sensitivity**: Highest; avoid excessive fat or fiber
- **Fluid**: 400–600 mL/hour depending on heat and sweat rate
- **Key risk**: Starting too aggressively on calories before the gut warms up

### Phase 2: Hours 3–8 (Mid-Race / Rhythm Phase)
- **Priority**: Carbohydrates + electrolytes; introduce some real food
- **Format**: Mix of gels/chews AND solid food (bars, PB&J, waffles, rice balls)
- **Target**: 200–300 cal/hour; 70–80g carbs/hour
- **Palatability shift**: Start craving salty/savory foods; rotate flavors to avoid sweetness fatigue
- **Key action**: Eat at every aid station even if not hungry yet

### Phase 3: Hours 8–20 (Late Race / Fatigue Phase)
- **Priority**: Mixed macros — fat and protein become essential alongside carbs
- **Format**: Real food strongly preferred; savory > sweet
- **Target**: 200–300 cal/hour; 60–80g carbs; add fat (cheese, PB, chips)
- **Research finding**: 100-mile finishers consumed ~5x more fat than non-finishers during this phase
- **Key risk**: Nausea, food aversion, inability to eat → race-ending if untreated

### Phase 4: Night Sections (Hours 10–30)
- **Priority**: Warm food + caffeine management
- **Format**: Hot soup/broth, ramen, warm potatoes, caffeinated gels/chews
- **Cal/hour may drop**: Stomach slows at night; focus on staying ahead of deficit
- **Caffeine**: 100–200mg caffeine every 3–4 hours; do not overdo (GI + anxiety)
- **Key action**: Eat something at every night aid station regardless of appetite

### Phase 5: Final Push (Last 10–25% of race)
- **Priority**: Carbohydrates + caffeinated products
- **Format**: Gels, chews, liquid calories (easiest to consume when exhausted)
- **Target**: Maintain 200+ cal/hour if possible; any calories count
- **Key risk**: Glycogen depletion → bonk; hyponatremia if drinking too much plain water

## The Gut Training Principle

The gut is trainable. Athletes who practice race-pace nutrition in training can tolerate significantly more carbohydrates without GI distress on race day.

**Training protocol**:
1. Practice eating at race pace on long runs (not just easy recovery runs)
2. Gradually increase carb intake per hour over weeks/months
3. Use the same products in training that you plan to use in the race
4. Practice nighttime eating (if doing a night-section race)

## Electrolytes in Ultra Running

| Electrolyte | Loss Rate (sweat) | Race Target | Sources |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sodium | 500–1500mg/L sweat | 500–1000mg/hour | Salt tabs, electrolyte mix, real food |
| Potassium | 150–500mg/L | 200–400mg/hour | Bananas, electrolyte mix |
| Magnesium | 10–40mg/L | 20–80mg/hour | Electrolyte mix, nuts |
| Calcium | 20–60mg/L | 40–120mg/hour | Dairy, electrolyte mix |

**Sodium is critical**: Low sodium (hyponatremia) is more dangerous than dehydration in ultras. Do not drink excessive plain water without matching sodium intake.

**Individual sweat rate varies enormously**: Some athletes lose 2000mg sodium/hour; others lose 400mg. Consider a Precision Hydration sweat test for personalized data.

## Common Nutrition Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Under-eating early (saving stomach for later) | Progressive glycogen depletion; bonk at mile 60 | Eat on a schedule from mile 1 |
| Eating only sweet foods for 24+ hours | Flavor aversion; inability to eat | Introduce savory foods after mile 30 |
| Drinking plain water without sodium | Hyponatremia (dangerous); bloating | Always use electrolyte mix or salt tabs |
| Eating too much fiber/fat at high intensity | GI distress; nausea; DNF | Reserve high-fat/fiber foods for walking |
| Skipping aid station food when not hungry | Falling behind on calories; crash later | "Eat by the clock, not by hunger" |
| Caffeine overload | Anxiety; GI cramping; heart rate issues | Cap at ~400mg/day; taper toward end |

## What Elite Ultra Runners Eat

### Jim Walmsley
- **Daily diet**: Vegetarian-leaning; yogurt + granola for breakfast; pizza 4–5x/week; occasional Ben & Jerry's when losing weight
- **Race target**: 300–400 cal/hour; primarily sports drinks + energy gels
- **Key lesson**: "Staying on top of calorific intake" is one of the biggest factors in race completion
- **Source**: [Sports Illustrated, 2020](https://www.si.com/edge/2020/02/06/jim-walmsley-ultrarunner-diet-nutrition)

### Courtney Dauwalter
- **Daily diet**: Self-described "normal person's diet" — no dietary restrictions; beer, nachos, candy corn
- **Race nutrition**: Highly disciplined mid-race plan; primarily **liquid calories** (Tailwind mix), plus gels, waffles, and chews
- **Philosophy**: Listens to cravings; believes restriction creates more problems than it solves
- **Caffeine strategy**: Uses caffeinated products strategically during night sections
- **Source**: [Eat for Endurance: Courtney Dauwalter Nutrition](https://www.eatforendurance.com/post/courtney-dauwalter-nutrition)

### Kilian Jornet
- **Daily diet**: High in whole foods; has shared detailed training/nutrition logs from 2022 season
- **Race nutrition**: Mix of gels/energy products and real food; avocado referenced in nutrition data
- **Altitude note**: Appetite suppression at altitude forces liquid-calorie focus
- **Source**: [Performance Gains Nutrition: Kilian Jornet Analysis](https://performancegainsnutrition.com/blog/killian-jornets-nutrition-what-we-can-learn-from-his-data)

### General Elite Patterns
- Elites consume more calories per hour than average runners (~333 vs. ~250 cal/hour)
- Use primarily liquid + gel sources for speed/convenience
- More experienced gut training allows higher carb absorption
- Greater variety of real food in 100-mile+ efforts vs. 50K/50-mile distances
- Many use sports dietitians to develop individualized plans

## Gut Friendliness Classification

### Easy to Digest (any intensity)
- Energy gels (especially Maurten, SiS isotonic, Huma)
- Drink mixes (Tailwind, Maurten)
- Gummy bears / candy
- Bananas
- White rice / rice balls
- Plain boiled potatoes
- Broth

### Moderate (best at easy pace or walking)
- Energy bars (Larabar, Honey Stinger Waffle)
- PB&J sandwich halves
- Saltines / pretzels
- Chips
- Quesadilla

### Hard to Digest (aid station / rest only)
- High-fat foods: cheese, bacon, pizza, full nut butter packets
- High-fiber foods: whole grain bread, Bobo's, vegetables
- High-protein foods: jerky, hard-boiled eggs
- Full meals: soup, mac & cheese, rice bowls

## Hydration Strategy

- **Minimum**: 400 mL/hour (cooler conditions / low sweat rate)
- **Standard**: 500–750 mL/hour (moderate conditions)
- **Hot conditions**: 750–1000+ mL/hour
- **Rule of thumb**: Urine should be pale yellow; dark yellow = dehydrated; clear = possibly overhydrated
- **Electrolyte mix**: Always use at ≥500 mL/hour intake
- **Plain water**: Safe for small sips; dangerous as sole fluid source in hot long events

## Race Day Fueling Plan Template

```
Pre-race (2 hours before):
- 400–600 cal; high carb, low fiber, low fat
- Examples: bagel + PB, oatmeal, banana + sports drink

First 30 minutes:
- Start fueling even if not hungry
- 1 gel or 2–3 chews every 20–30 min

Hours 1–6:
- 1 gel per 30–45 min, OR
- 1 bar per 45–60 min, OR
- 150–200 mL Tailwind/Maurten per 20–30 min
- Match with electrolyte drink

Hours 6–12:
- Rotate to real food at aid stations
- Add salty/savory foods
- Reduce reliance on sweet gels alone
- Hot soup/broth at night aid stations

Hours 12+:
- Stomach first — anything you can eat counts
- Caffeinated products if sleepy
- Warm foods preferred at night
- Liquid calories if solid food becoming difficult
```
